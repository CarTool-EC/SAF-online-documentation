Usage Guidance and Methodology for an Intervention

Show how to use the RA in an intervention.

The use of reference architectures within an intervention directly translates policy objectives into structured, interoperable solution designs, reducing fragmentation and increasing consistency across public services. At the core of this approach, the European Interoperability Reference Architecture (EIRA©) functions as the analytical backbone, enabling stakeholders to systematically assess requirements across legal, organisational, semantic, and technical dimensions. This ensures that all relevant constraints and dependencies are identified early, mitigating the risk of misalignment between policy intent and operational delivery. Building on this analytical foundation, the eGovernment Reference Architecture (eGovERA©) extends the process into the design and operational domain.

By introducing greater granularity and domain-specific guidance, it supports decision-making and portfolio management, allowing administrations to move from conceptual alignment to actionable solution design. This progression is critical for maintaining coherence while enabling flexibility in implementation. A central mechanism in this process is the traceability between Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) and Solution Building Blocks (SBBs). ABBs define normative requirements derived from policy and interoperability needs, while SBBs translate these requirements into implementable components or standards to be used. This traceability ensures that every element of the final design can be directly linked back to its originating requirement, reducing ambiguity and supporting accountability.

In practice, ABBs cover elements such as applicable legislation, organisational capabilities, data structures, and technical services, while SBBs operationalise these into legal agreements, governance models, datasets, and application interfaces. Alignment with the European Interoperability Framework ensures that this structured approach supports seamless cross-border data exchange, reducing duplication and enabling scalable digital public services.

Definition and Purpose of an Intervention

An intervention serves as a targeted mechanism to translate policy objectives into interoperable digital public services, ensuring that interoperability is embedded from the outset rather than retrofitted later. It is defined as a pilot activity under Policy Support Implementation that applies structured reference architectures to guide the definition and design of such services. The primary purpose of an intervention is to enable seamless data exchange between public administrations, businesses, and citizens by addressing interoperability across legal, organisational, semantic, and technical dimensions. This comprehensive scope reduces the risk of isolated or incompatible solutions, which can lead to inefficiencies, increased costs, and barriers to cross-border collaboration. The central unit of analysis within an intervention is the digital public service, understood as a service delivered by public administrations to achieve specific policy goals through information and communication technologies. By focusing on this unit, the intervention maintains a clear link between policy intent and service delivery outcomes. Interventions are deliberately constrained in scope and duration, typically lasting around three months. This limited timeframe ensures rapid progress while focusing on producing a partial yet reusable design model rather than a full implementation. While this accelerates delivery, it also introduces the risk that incomplete specifications may require further refinement during subsequent implementation phases. The outputs of an intervention include a clearly defined digital public service, a structured set of identified requirements, and a partial solution model expressed using standardised modelling approaches. These outputs provide a reusable foundation that can be extended by public administrations, supporting consistency and reducing duplication across future initiatives.

Overall Process of an Intervention

The intervention process is structured to ensure that policy intent is consistently translated into implementable solutions, reducing fragmentation across jurisdictions. It begins with legislation, which establishes the regulatory foundation and defines the objectives that the digital public service must fulfil. From this starting point, a co-creation model is applied, enabling collaboration between central authorities and Member States. In this model, the European Commission provides initial requirements and a partial design, which ensures alignment with established interoperability standards. Member States then complete and implement the solution, allowing for adaptation to national contexts while maintaining overall consistency. This division of responsibilities balances standardisation with flexibility but requires careful coordination to avoid divergence during implementation. Execution is inherently multidisciplinary. Business-oriented teams focus on identifying needs, capabilities, and legal and organisational requirements, ensuring that the service aligns with policy and operational realities. In parallel, technical teams define the data structures and technical components, translating requirements into design models and implementable elements. This collaboration is essential to avoid gaps between policy design and technical execution.

Interventions address multiple dimensions of interoperability, including governance structures, data alignment, and technical integration. This comprehensive approach reduces the risk of isolated solutions that fail to integrate across systems or jurisdictions. Supporting tools play a critical role in maintaining consistency and quality. Modelling tools and interoperability test environments enable validation, reuse, and alignment with reference architectures. These tools help ensure that outputs are not only theoretically sound but also practically applicable, thereby increasing the likelihood of successful implementation.

Lifecycle Phases of an Intervention

The intervention follows a structured lifecycle composed of five phases: Inception, Definition, Analysis, Design, and Share. This phased approach provides a clear progression from initial engagement to delivery and ensures that each stage builds on validated outputs from the previous one. It also introduces control points that support stakeholder alignment and quality assurance.

Phase 1 – Inception: Establishing alignment and commitment

The initial phase establishes the strategic foundation necessary for a successful intervention by ensuring that the public administration recognises the value of adopting structured approaches to analysing and designing Digital Public Services. This alignment is critical, as early misunderstanding or lack of commitment would undermine downstream activities and delay outcomes. The process begins with a formal kick-off meeting that clarifies objectives, methodology, and expected timelines, creating transparency and securing agreement to proceed with a pilot intervention. This is followed by a structured onboarding session that explains the architectural frameworks, including their organisation into distinct views and components, enabling stakeholders to understand how these frameworks support interoperability and service design. Engagement is intentionally focused on senior decision-makers to ensure institutional backing, while the intervention team simultaneously fulfils a commercial function, securing participation, and a training function, building foundational knowledge. This dual role mitigates the risk of misalignment between strategic intent and technical execution.

Phase 2 – Definition: Scoping the intervention and formalising requirements

The definition phase translates strategic intent into a clearly bounded and agreed scope, reducing ambiguity and preventing scope creep during later stages. It focuses on articulating the specific use case, including its objectives, involved actors, and sequence of interactions, establishing a shared understanding of the service to be developed or improved. This clarity enables the identification of Digital Business Capabilities and Digital Public Services, alongside the functional and technical requirements necessary to support them. These elements are validated with stakeholders to ensure completeness and relevance, minimising the risk of rework. By aligning requirements with established architectural catalogues, the phase ensures that interoperability considerations are embedded from the outset rather than retrofitted later, which would increase cost and complexity. For example, identifying capabilities such as reporting cross-border transactions and services such as secure authentication demonstrates how business needs are directly linked to enabling functionalities.

Phase 3 – Analysis: Translating requirements into architecture building blocks

The analysis phase converts defined requirements into structured architectural components, enabling traceability and consistency across the solution. This step is essential for reducing implementation risk, as it ensures that all requirements are systematically addressed within a coherent framework. Architecture Building Blocks are identified across legal, organisational, semantic, and technical perspectives, each representing a set of normative requirements aligned with interoperability principles. Legal elements ensure compliance with applicable legislation, while organisational components define roles, services, and information management responsibilities. Semantic elements establish a common understanding of data through shared definitions and formats, and technical components specify the services and interfaces required to operationalise the solution. These elements are consolidated into an initial architecture model, providing a structured representation that links business needs to architectural components. This traceability enables early validation with stakeholders, reducing the likelihood of misinterpretation and ensuring alignment before progressing to detailed design.

Phase 4 – Design: Developing the solution architecture model

The design phase transforms abstract architectural components into concrete, implementable solutions, in so doing bridging the gap between concept and execution. This progression reduces uncertainty by defining how each requirement will be realised in practice. Architecture Building Blocks are refined into Solution Building Blocks, which represent tangible elements such as specific legal instruments, governance arrangements, datasets, and technical services. Legal components define the exact regulatory frameworks governing the service, while organisational elements establish operational agreements and governance structures necessary for delivery. Semantic components specify the datasets, formats, and agreements required for consistent data exchange, and technical components define the services and specifications that enable interoperability. The resulting model provides a detailed and actionable blueprint, ensuring that all elements are aligned with recognised standards. Validation with stakeholders at this stage ensures feasibility and acceptance, mitigating the risk of implementation delays or redesign.

Phase 5 – Share: Handover, communication, and closure

The final phase ensures that the outcomes of the intervention are effectively transferred, understood, and positioned for implementation, safeguarding the value generated throughout the process. A structured handover presents the final design model alongside the remaining steps required for execution, ensuring continuity and clarity of responsibility. A closing presentation consolidates deliverables and timelines, reinforcing stakeholder alignment and readiness for implementation. The production of a final report and supporting materials enables broader communication and reuse, extending the impact beyond the initial intervention. At this stage, any outstanding elements, such as the completion or alignment of datasets and semantic agreements, are clearly identified to avoid implementation gaps. By disseminating results through shared platforms, the phase supports knowledge reuse and transparency, reducing duplication of effort and enhancing long-term interoperability across administrations.

Roles in the Intervention Process

The intervention requires a multidisciplinary team that combines business and technical expertise. This reflects the dual nature of interoperable digital public services, which must align policy objectives with technical implementation. The business team is responsible for identifying needs and defining capabilities, services, and requirements. It also maps legal and organisational Architecture and Solution Building Blocks, ensuring alignment with policy and governance structures. The technical team translates these requirements into formal models and proposes semantic and technical solutions. It develops the analysis and design models using ArchiMate and ensures that technical components are coherent and aligned with requirements.

Effective collaboration between these profiles is essential. A disconnect between business and technical perspectives can lead to incomplete or impractical designs. Conversely, strong integration supports the development of solutions that are both technically sound and operationally viable.

Align RA with portfolio management and strategy planning.

The eGovERA Solution Architecture Framework (SAF) adopts a problem-solving paradigm, structuring the implementation lifecycle around distinct use cases. The primary use cases are the analysis of the problem, where requirements and constraints are defined, and the solution design, where alternative solutions are developed in terms of Solution Building Blocks supported by specifications and standards. Additional use cases extend the lifecycle to include testing, realisation, and composition or integration. This broader scope ensures that implementation is not limited to design but includes validation and deployment activities. The Architecture Implementation Method (AIM) within eGovERA organises these use cases as phases within a graph structure that allows multiple execution sequences. This approach provides flexibility in aligning architecture work with portfolio management and strategic planning.
