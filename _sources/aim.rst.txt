eGovERA Architecture Implementation Method
==========================================

Analysis of the target Interoperable Solution
---------------------------------------------

The analysis phase encompasses the identification of digital business
capabilities, the definition and documentation of high-level and
low-level requirements, the establishment of traceability between these
requirements, and their validation. It also includes the identification
and validation of business principles that guide architectural
decisions. Each of these activities contributes to a structured and
verifiable foundation for subsequent design.

Effective interoperability and coherent solution delivery depend on the
establishment of a shared understanding of requirements, terminology,
and architectural intent. Without a common terminology, public
administrations risk misalignment, ambiguity in communication, and
inefficiencies in the design and assessment of Interoperable Solutions.
To mitigate these risks, requirement elicitation must formalise a
consistent vocabulary that supports clear communication across
stakeholders and ensures that architectural decisions remain traceable
to policy objectives. This process relies on defining required
capabilities and requirements as Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs).
ABBs capture requirements as reusable capabilities, enabling consistency
and reuse across solutions while also including key requirements
necessary to ensure interoperability. The analysis must consider
requirements across legal, organisational, semantic, and technical
dimensions (LOST), documenting which ABBs are required within each view
to realise the intended capabilities. This multi-view assessment ensures
that no critical dimension of interoperability is omitted, reducing the
risk of non-compliance or fragmented analysis.

To maintain alignment with public policy objectives and interoperability
principles, motivation and principles views are used to connect digital
business capabilities, digital public services, and application
services. This traceability ensures that architectural decisions are not
isolated technical constructs but are instead grounded in policy intent
and operational needs. 

Validation of requirements is achieved through conformance testing,
which ensures that defined requirements are both complete and consistent
with the architectural framework. This can be implemented using the
Interoperability Test Bed, a service offered by the European
Commission’s Directorate-General for Informatics (DIGIT). The Test Bed
supports conformance testing at both technical and business levels,
allowing detailed diagnostics when failures occur while maintaining
abstraction when appropriate. The validation process is further
supported by the EIRA Validator, a public validation service hosted
within the Test Bed that provides predefined validation profiles for
different types of European Interoperability Reference Architecture
(EIRA) models.

Design of the target Interoperable Solution 
--------------------------------------------

The ability to translate defined requirements into implementable
solutions directly affects the feasibility, interoperability, and
sustainability of the architecture. If this translation is not properly
achieved, there is a risk of divergence between intended requirements
and implemented solutions, leading to inconsistencies and reduced
interoperability. In the design phase, Solution Building Blocks (SBBs)
represent the concrete implementation of the previously defined
Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs). For each required ABB, candidate
SBBs must be identified, including products, services, standards, and
specifications. This identification process is supported by the European
Library of Interoperability Specifications (ELIS) and by the established
links between ABBs and specifications within the European
Interoperability Reference Architecture (EIRA).

Conformance testing remains a critical control mechanism during the
design phase to ensure that the selected SBBs align with the defined
ABBs. This is achieved through the application of eGovERA Validator
Rules (as part of EIRA Validator), which verify that the ABBs and SBBs
used in the solution correspond to those defined in the reference model.
Additionally, these rules ensure that SBBs correctly reference the ABBs
they implement, preserving traceability between requirements and
solutions.

Failure to enforce this alignment introduces risks related to
architectural drift, where implemented solutions deviate from the
defined architecture, potentially compromising interoperability,
compliance, and maintainability. Therefore, the design phase must
rigorously maintain consistency between architectural intent and
implementation choices, ensuring that all components contribute
coherently to the overall solution architecture.
